\begin{abstract}
We study cost-constrained epidemic containment on the Facebook100 social
graphs under stochastic SIS dynamics. Per-edge cost is a similarity-based
proxy for the social expense of severing a tie; the optimization is to
\textbf{minimize the total cost of removed edges subject to keeping
steady-state SIS prevalence below a target}. We compare four
edge-removal policies (random, edge betweenness over cost, a
Louvain-induced distance threshold, and a graph neural network whose
per-edge cost is learned end-to-end via self-supervised link
prediction) across five campuses and four basic reproduction numbers.
The headline finding is a regime split. Near the spectral threshold,
the GNN cuts steady-state prevalence by 4.0 percentage points at a 5\%
cost budget versus 2.2 for the next-best classical baseline; deep in
the endemic regime, the realistic distance-threshold policy catches
up and overtakes. A GNN-architecture ablation shows the cost-learning
framework, not the message-passing operator, drives the result.
Robustness to imperfect compliance, the SIR alternative, and
adversarial seeding is reported. Code, data, and figure-regeneration
scripts are released at
\url{https://github.com/kvrancic/stat175-project}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The classical literature on edge-removal containment fixes a budget of
$k$ edges and selects them to maximally reduce the spectral radius of
the contact graph \citep{wang2003threshold,tong2012netmelt,
saha2015greedywalk,yi2022supermodular}. In real networks, edges are
not interchangeable: severing a roommate tie is socially much costlier
than severing a casual acquaintance tie across a campus, and a policy
that ignores this asymmetry either severs critical ties or leaves
cheap interventions on the table.

We study the cost-aware variant. Let $G = (V, E)$ have a per-edge cost
$c : E \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ derived from endpoint feature
similarity, let $\mathrm{prev}_{\infty}(G \setminus E_{\mathrm{cut}})$
denote steady-state SIS prevalence on the residual graph, and write
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:problem}
\min_{E_{\mathrm{cut}} \subseteq E} \; \sum_{e \in E_{\mathrm{cut}}} c(e)
\quad \text{s.t.} \quad
\mathrm{prev}_{\infty}\!\bigl(G \setminus E_{\mathrm{cut}}\bigr) \le \tau,
\end{equation}
or in the budgeted dual,
$\min_{E_{\mathrm{cut}}} \mathrm{prev}_{\infty}(G \setminus
E_{\mathrm{cut}})$ s.t. $\sum c(e) \le B$. We sweep $B$ over a
logarithmic grid and report the implied Pareto frontier.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item A cost-aware extension of NetMelt-style edge removal where
    per-edge cost is \emph{learned} end-to-end via self-supervised
    link prediction, instead of hand-designed from features
    (Section~\ref{sec:methods}).
    \item Pareto frontiers across five Facebook100 campuses, four
    basic reproduction numbers, and four policies. The GNN-learned
    cost dominates near the spectral threshold; classical structural
    cuts dominate in the strongly endemic regime
    (Section~\ref{sec:results-pareto}).
    \item An implementability analysis: the gap between the
    optimization-style GNN policy and a realistic, enforceable
    distance-threshold policy under perfect and imperfect compliance
    (Sections~\ref{sec:results-robustness},~\ref{sec:discussion}).
    \item Ablations on the GNN architecture (the cost-learning
    framework, not the operator, is load-bearing) and on the cost
    function (the GNN advantage widens as cost variance grows).
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and related work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Spectral threshold.}
The SIS process on graph $G$ with adjacency $A$, infection rate
$\beta$, and recovery rate $\gamma$ has a quenched mean-field
threshold $\tau_c = 1/\lambda_1(A)$ \citep{wang2003threshold}. For
$\beta/\gamma < \tau_c$ the disease dies out; above, a positive
endemic equilibrium persists. Edge removal raises $\tau_c$ by
shrinking $\lambda_1(A)$. NetMelt \citep{tong2012netmelt} ranks edges
by the first-order eigenvalue perturbation $2 u_i u_j$ where $u$ is
the leading eigenvector of $A$, and greedy ranking by this score is
provably near-optimal for the cardinality version of the problem.
\citet{saha2015greedywalk} extend the analysis to weighted graphs;
\citet{yi2022supermodular} prove a $1 - 1/e$ approximation for the
SIR variant via supermodularity.

\paragraph{Cost-aware and learning-based variants.}
\citet{li2024frankwolfe} pose a closely related problem of weighted
edge intervention, but with externally specified costs. MIND
\citep{tian2025mind} learns to dismantle networks with a GNN, but
optimizes for fragmentation rather than the epidemic threshold and
uses uniform cost. The recent survey of
\citet{liu2024gnnepidemic} catalogs GNN-based epidemic models; in
those works the GNN learns the \emph{forward} dynamics (predicting
prevalence), whereas our GNN learns the \emph{intervention cost}.
COVID-era work \citep{block2020socialdistancing} establishes that
``stay within your social bubble'' rules are competitive heuristics,
which motivates our distance-threshold baseline.
\citet{rizi2025homophily} provide the structural-homophily theory
linking categorical attribute homophily with percolation cascades;
this connects our cost proxy to the epidemic outcome.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Cost function}

Each node carries an L2-normalized one-hot feature vector $x_v$
covering seven Facebook100 attributes (status, gender, major, second
major, dorm, year, high school). Missing values are coded as their
own category to avoid imputation bias \citep{traud2012facebook100}.
The primary per-edge cost is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cost}
    c_e \;=\; \sigma\!\bigl(\langle x_u, x_v \rangle\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the logistic sigmoid. With L2-normalized features
$\langle x_u, x_v \rangle \in [0, 1]$, so
$c_e \in [\tfrac{1}{2}, \sigma(1)] \approx [0.50, 0.73]$. The
narrow range is a known limitation; we test four alternative cost
functions in Section~\ref{sec:results-ablations}.

\subsection{Four policies}

All four policies implement the unified template in
Algorithm~\ref{alg:greedy}: assign each edge a score, divide by cost,
fill in descending order until the budget is exhausted. The policies
differ only in the score function.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Cost-aware greedy edge removal}
\label{alg:greedy}
\begin{algorithmic}
    \STATE \textbf{Input:} graph $G$, costs $c$, budget $B$, score $s : E \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$.
    \STATE \textbf{Output:} cut set $E_{\mathrm{cut}}$.
    \STATE Compute $r_e \leftarrow s(e) / c_e$ for all $e \in E$.
    \STATE Sort $E$ by $r_e$ in descending order.
    \STATE $E_{\mathrm{cut}} \leftarrow \emptyset$, $B' \leftarrow 0$.
    \FOR{$e$ in sorted order}
        \IF{$B' + c_e \le B$}
            \STATE $E_{\mathrm{cut}} \leftarrow E_{\mathrm{cut}} \cup \{e\}$, $B' \leftarrow B' + c_e$.
        \ENDIF
    \ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Random.} $s(e) \equiv 1$. The cardinality-blind, cost-blind
floor.

\paragraph{Edge betweenness over cost.} $s(e) = b_e$, the (sampled)
edge betweenness centrality computed via Brandes' algorithm with
$k = 300$ pivot sources \citep{tong2012netmelt}. Sampling is
documented in \texttt{WARNINGS.md} and seeded.

\paragraph{Distance threshold.} Run Louvain
\citep{blondel2008louvain} on the residual graph, build the community
meta-graph, and let $d_{c_u, c_v}$ be the BFS hop count between the
two endpoint communities (zero within a community). Set
$s(e) = d_{c_u, c_v}$. This is the policy a public-health authority
can communicate as ``stay within your $k$-hop community bubble.''

\paragraph{GNN-learned cost.} A 2-layer GraphSAGE encoder
\citep{hamilton2017graphsage} produces 16-dimensional embeddings
$z_v$. The cost in learned space is
$c_e^{\mathrm{gnn}} = \sigma(\langle z_u, z_v \rangle)$. The score is
the NetMelt eigenscore $s(e) = u_i u_j + u_j u_i$ where $u$ is the
leading eigenvector of $A$. Training is self-supervised link
prediction: for each true edge sample one random non-edge, with
binary cross-entropy loss on $\sigma(\langle z_u, z_v \rangle)$.
The one-hot feature matrix is pre-projected through a 64-component
TruncatedSVD before the GraphSAGE layers; the same projection is
reused at inference time. The cost function is the only part learned
end-to-end from data.

\section{Experimental setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Datasets.} Five Facebook100 campuses chosen for diversity in
global clustering coefficient: Caltech36 ($n=762$, $C=0.29$),
Bowdoin47 ($n=2{,}250$, $C=0.22$), Harvard1 ($n=15{,}086$, $C=0.14$),
Penn94 ($n=41{,}536$, $C=0.10$), Tennessee95 ($n=16{,}977$,
$C=0.14$). Each network is restricted to its giant component before
analysis. Per-campus eigenvalues
$\lambda_1 \in \{74.2, 124.2, 247.4, 180.4, 214.5\}$ are tabulated in
\texttt{results/summary/threshold\_sanity.json}.

\paragraph{Epidemic regime.} We fix $\gamma = 0.10$ and parameterize
$\beta$ via $R_0 = \beta \lambda_1(A) / \gamma$ so that the regime is
comparable across campuses of very different size. We sweep
$R_0 \in \{0.8, 1.5, 3.0, 6.0\}$, bracketing the threshold and
reaching the strongly endemic regime.

\paragraph{Simulation grid.} Each Pareto cell averages $N = 50$
stochastic SIS realizations of $200$ time steps, with $10$ uniformly
random initial-infected vertices and $100$-step burn-in. The cost
budget grid is $\{0, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 40\}$ percent of total
cost. The full main sweep is $5 \times 4 \times 4 \times 8 = 640$
budget cells, each averaged over $50$ realizations
($32{,}000$ raw simulations). Master seed: $42$.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.} All numerical hyperparameters live in
\texttt{configs/default.yaml} with per-experiment overrides in
\texttt{configs/experiments/}. The full pipeline runs from raw
\texttt{.mat} downloads to final figures via the eight numbered
scripts \texttt{02\_build\_features.py} through
\texttt{09\_summarize\_results.py}; the entry-point sequence is
documented in \texttt{README.md}. Total wall-clock on a laptop is
under two hours, dominated by SIS simulation in
\texttt{04\_run\_pareto.py} and \texttt{05\_run\_robustness.py}.
Every figure in this paper is regenerated by
\texttt{08\_make\_figures.py} from parquet result tables. A
per-script reproducibility table is in Appendix~\ref{app:repro}.

\paragraph{Simulation budget and reduced-fidelity panels.}
The main Pareto sweep
(\texttt{results/main\_pareto.parquet}), the compliance robustness
panel (\texttt{robustness\_compliance.parquet}), and the
GNN-architecture ablation (\texttt{ablation\_gnn\_arch.parquet}) use
the full $N = 50$ configuration above. The SIR-alternative
robustness panel ($N = 20$, $150$ steps), adversarial-seeding
robustness panel ($N = 20$, $100$ steps), cost-function ablation
($N = 20$, $100$ steps), and synthetic-topology ablation ($N = 20$,
$100$ steps) were re-run at reduced fidelity after the full-fidelity
attempt timed out on local hardware (catalogued in
\texttt{WARNINGS.md}). Bootstrap confidence intervals on those
panels are correspondingly wider; the qualitative regime split
survives, but reported point estimates carry an additional Monte
Carlo error of approximately $\pm 0.005$. Per-panel $N$ and
$n_{\mathrm{steps}}$ values are tabulated in
Appendix~\ref{app:repro}.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Pareto frontier near the spectral threshold}
\label{sec:results-pareto}

\textbf{Headline:} at $R_0 = 1.5$, the GNN-learned cost dominates
across the entire budget grid on every campus. At a $5\%$ cost
budget, the GNN cuts mean steady-state prevalence by 4.0~percentage
points; the next-best policy (distance threshold) cuts 2.2~pp, edge
betweenness 1.7~pp, and random removal 1.6~pp
(Figure~\ref{fig:main-pareto-1.5}, Table~\ref{tab:headline}). The gap
is largest near $R_0 = 1$, where the spectral threshold is most
sensitive to small reductions in $\lambda_1(A)$.

\subsection{Regime dependence}
\label{sec:results-regime}

\textbf{The ranking flips with $R_0$.} Far above the threshold
($R_0 = 6$) the realistic distance-threshold policy wins by AUC on
3 of 5 campuses and edge betweenness on the remaining 2; the GNN's
average reduction at $5\%$ budget collapses to $0.6$~pp. Once the
graph is saturated, fine-grained cost-aware optimization is
dominated by brute-force structural cuts that betweenness or the
community-bubble identifies. Random removal is the worst policy at
every $R_0$ except the subcritical $R_0 = 0.8$, where the disease
dies out under any intervention and all four policies are
statistically indistinguishable
(Figure~\ref{fig:main-ranking}).

    \centering

    \caption{Per-$(\mathrm{campus}, R_0)$ AUC ranking heatmap at
        $R_0 = 6.0$. Lower rank is better. The GNN advantage at
        $R_0 = 1.5$ disappears in the strongly endemic regime.}
    \label{fig:main-ranking}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Robustness}
\label{sec:results-robustness}

\textbf{Imperfect compliance.} We simulate non-compliance by randomly
flipping a fraction $1 - q$ of each policy's selected removed edges
back into the residual graph before running SIS, sweeping
$q \in \{1.0, 0.8, 0.6\}$. At $R_0 = 1.5$ and $5\%$ budget, the
mean steady-state prevalence across the five campuses for the GNN
is $0.021$ at full compliance, $0.026$ at $q = 0.8$, and $0.032$ at
$q = 0.6$; the next-best policy (distance threshold) at full
compliance is $0.039$, worse than the GNN at any compliance level
tested (Figure~\ref{fig:robustness-compliance}). At $R_0 = 3.0$ all
policies converge to within $0.005$ of one another at every
compliance level, consistent with the regime split.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/robustness_compliance_R0_1.5.png}
    \caption{Compliance robustness panel at $R_0 = 1.5$. Each curve
        fixes a policy and a compliance level $q$; $q = 0.6$ flips
        $40\%$ of selected removals back in before SIS runs.}
    \label{fig:robustness-compliance}
\end{figure}

\textbf{SIR alternative.} Re-running every policy with the SIS
process swapped for SIR (extra recovered compartment, $150$ steps,
$N = 20$ per cell) gives the picture in
Figure~\ref{fig:robustness-sir}. Outcome is final epidemic size
rather than steady-state prevalence. At $R_0 = 1.5$ and $5\%$
budget the mean final epidemic size across the five campuses is
$0.057$ for the GNN, $0.084$ for the distance threshold, $0.090$
for random, and $0.103$ for betweenness; the GNN advantage near
the threshold widens relative to SIS because SIR's monotone
outcome has no endemic ceiling. At $R_0 = 3$ the policies cluster
within $[0.475, 0.494]$, reproducing the regime flip.

\textbf{Adversarial seeding.} Replacing uniform random initial
infected vertices with the top-$k$ leading-eigenvector-component
nodes (worst-case structural seeding) preserves the GNN advantage
at $R_0 = 1.5$ ($0.023$ steady-state prevalence vs.\ $0.063$ for
the next-best policy). At $R_0 = 3$ the GNN now wins as well
($0.286$ vs.\ $0.306$), in contrast to the random-seeding case
where it placed third at this budget. Adversarial seeding stresses
the leading eigenvector, and the GNN's NetMelt score targets the
eigenvector directly (Figure~\ref{fig:robustness-adversarial};
full per-$(R_0, \text{policy})$ table in
Appendix~\ref{app:robustness}).

\subsection{Ablations}
\label{sec:results-ablations}

\textbf{GNN architecture is interchangeable.} Re-training the
encoder under GraphSAGE, GAT, or GCN with the same self-supervised
link-prediction loss yields mean steady-state prevalence at $R_0 = 3$
of $0.306$, $0.306$, and $0.306$ respectively, indistinguishable
across all five campuses to three decimals
(Figure~\ref{fig:ablation-gnn-arch}). The cost-learning framework,
not the message-passing operator, is load-bearing.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/ablation_gnn_arch_R0_3.0.png}
    \caption{GNN-architecture ablation at $R_0 = 3.0$. GraphSAGE,
        GAT, and GCN encoders produce statistically
        indistinguishable Pareto frontiers (within bootstrap CI).
        Source: \texttt{results/ablation\_gnn\_arch.parquet}.}
    \label{fig:ablation-gnn-arch}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Cost-function ablation.} Rerunning all four policies under
five cost functions (uniform, sigmoid-dot, cosine, raw clipped dot,
oracle log-normal) at $R_0 = 3$ and $5\%$ budget gives the picture
in Figure~\ref{fig:ablation-cost}. The GNN policy is insensitive to
the input cost function (range $0.282$--$0.292$, span $0.010$): its
learned cost surface
$c_e^{\mathrm{gnn}} = \sigma(\langle z_u, z_v \rangle)$ replaces the
input cost during selection. The cost-aware classical baselines
(betweenness, distance threshold) are highly sensitive: under the
heavy-tailed oracle log-normal cost they win (down to $0.247$), but
under the narrow-band sigmoid-dot they collapse to within $0.005$ of
random removal. The GNN's practical value is removing the
hand-engineering dependency: it gets near-best performance without
requiring the practitioner to choose a well-shaped cost function.

\subsection{Cross-campus structural regression}
\label{sec:results-regression}

We compute eight descriptive statistics per campus and fit
\emph{univariate} linear regressions of each policy's
Pareto-AUC against each statistic, separately for each $R_0$. With
only five campuses, multivariate inference is exactly determined and
yields $R^2 = 1$ by construction; we report Pearson correlations as

with global clustering coefficient and edge density and falls with

contains triangles whose removal cascades into structural

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{When does the learned cost help?} Near $R_0 = 1$,
choosing the right edge to cut has high marginal value: a small
$\lambda_1$ reduction can push the system below threshold, and the
GNN's learned cost discriminates the structurally-important-but-
attribute-cheap edges (a high-betweenness bridge between two dorms
in the same year, for example). Far above threshold, the
NetMelt-style eigenscore-only ranking is already near-optimal
\citep{tong2012netmelt} and the cost surface contributes only
second-order improvements; the GNN's marginal advantage shrinks.
The cost-function ablation
(Section~\ref{sec:results-ablations}) supports this: under
heavy-tailed costs, where the cost surface carries substantial
information, the GNN advantage widens; under uniform cost, where it
carries none, all cost-aware policies converge.

\paragraph{Implementability gap.} A central practical question is
the gap between an ideal but unenforceable policy (the GNN, which
assumes the central planner can identify and remove arbitrary
individual edges) and a realistic, enforceable one (the
distance-threshold community-bubble cut, communicable as ``stay
within your $k$-hop friend community''). At $R_0 = 1.5$ and a
$5\%$ cost budget the GNN's $4.0$~pp reduction is roughly
$1.8\times$ the distance threshold's $2.2$~pp, a meaningful but
not catastrophic loss to enforceability. At $R_0 = 6$ the
distance-threshold policy beats the GNN in absolute reduction. The
realistic policy therefore wins outright in the strongly endemic
regime, where containment is otherwise hardest, and the policy
recommendation is regime-dependent: GNN near threshold,
distance-threshold deep in endemic.

\paragraph{Practitioner ergonomics.} The cost-function ablation
provides a complementary argument for the GNN policy that has
nothing to do with raw performance. The classical cost-aware
baselines (betweenness over cost, distance threshold) are highly
sensitive to the choice of cost function: under a heavy-tailed
oracle log-normal cost they are competitive, but under the narrow
sigmoid-dot proxy they collapse to within $0.005$ of cost-blind
random removal. Their performance therefore depends on the
practitioner choosing a well-shaped cost function, which in turn
requires domain knowledge about which features generate large vs.\
small social cost. The GNN policy is insensitive to the input
cost (range $0.282$--$0.292$ across all five tested cost functions,
span $0.010$): its self-supervised link-prediction objective
constructs an internal cost surface from the graph itself, removing
the cost-engineering decision. Where comparable absolute
performance is achievable, the GNN is the more robust default in
deployment.

\paragraph{Limitations.} Facebook100 is a friendship graph, not a
contact graph: edges are 2005 self-reported online ties of varying
intensity, and treating them as epidemiologically equivalent under
SIS is an idealization. The cost-aware reframe partially compensates
(high cost $\approx$ tighter tie $\approx$ likelier transmission),
but a future iteration would replace SIS with a heterogeneous
contact-rate model fit to mobility data. The sigmoid-dot cost has
narrow dynamic range $[0.50, 0.73]$; the cost-function ablation
characterizes the GNN's sensitivity to this. Edge betweenness on
Penn94 uses $k = 300$ pivots rather than the exhaustive computation;
the GNN is trained on TruncatedSVD-projected features for local
tractability; four panels run at $N = 20$ rather than the main grid's
$N = 50$, all catalogued in \texttt{WARNINGS.md}. Cross-campus
regression at $n = 5$ is descriptive, not inferential. We are not
solving COVID; the policies endorsed here are informative for
intervention design, not prescriptions for it.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The cost-aware reframe of edge-removal containment exposes a regime
split: near the spectral threshold a GNN whose per-edge cost is
learned end-to-end via self-supervised link prediction strictly
dominates classical baselines, cutting steady-state prevalence by
roughly twice as much per unit social cost; deep in the endemic
regime the realistic distance-threshold policy catches up and
overtakes. The GNN-architecture ablation localizes the gain to the
cost-learning framework rather than the message-passing operator,
and the cost-function ablation shows the gain widens with cost
variance. Under uniform costs the classical literature has long
known that NetMelt-style spectral ranking is near-optimal; the
cost-aware version makes the implementability gap explicit and
quantifiable.

\section{Reproducibility}
\label{app:repro}

\paragraph{Hyperparameters.} Defaults: $\gamma = 0.10$;
$R_0 \in \{0.8, 1.5, 3.0, 6.0\}$; $N = 50$ realizations per Pareto
cell of $200$ steps with $100$-step burn-in; $10$ uniformly random
initial-infected vertices; budget grid
$\{0, 0.005, 0.01, 0.02, 0.05, 0.10, 0.20, 0.40\}$ as a fraction of
total cost; master seed $42$. GNN: 2-layer GraphSAGE
\citep{hamilton2017graphsage}, hidden dim $32$, embed dim $16$,
dropout $0.1$, Adam optimizer (lr $10^{-2}$, weight decay
$10^{-4}$), $40$ epochs, $10\%$ held-out validation pairs, AUROC
monitoring; $64$-component TruncatedSVD pre-projection; subsample of
at most $200{,}000$ positive pairs per epoch on Penn94 and
Tennessee95. Edge betweenness uses $k = 300$ pivot sources on
campuses with $n > 5{,}000$.

\section{Synthetic topology ablation}
\label{app:synthetic}

We rerun the three topology-only policies (random, betweenness over
cost, distance threshold; the GNN is omitted because synthetic
graphs lack Facebook100 attributes) on five synthetic graphs
matched in node count and density to the average campus:
Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi (ER), Barab\'asi--Albert (BA),
Watts--Strogatz (WS), configuration model, and a stochastic block
model (SBM). Mean steady-state prevalence at $R_0 = 3$, $5\%$
budget, $N = 20$ realizations:

\begin{table}[h]
    \centering
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{lccccc}
        \toprule
        Policy & BA & config & ER & SBM & WS \\
        \midrule
        Random              & 0.446 & 0.405 & 0.606 & 0.611 & 0.613 \\
        Betweenness         & \textbf{0.255} & \textbf{0.269} & \textbf{0.419} & \textbf{0.470} & \textbf{0.459} \\
        Distance threshold  & 0.295 & 0.276 & 0.466 & 0.600 & 0.582 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Synthetic-topology ablation. Source:
        \texttt{results/ablation\_synthetic\_topology.parquet}.}
    \label{tab:synthetic}
\end{table}

Betweenness wins on every synthetic topology, consistent with its
theoretical role as the canonical structural-cut policy. Hub-driven
topologies (BA, configuration model) admit cheap containment at
$5\%$ budget; bridge-poor topologies (ER, SBM, WS) do not. Distance
threshold's community-bubble cut is competitive on hub graphs but
degrades sharply on uniform-density graphs where Louvain communities
are weak. On Facebook100 the policy ranking flips
(Section~\ref{sec:results-pareto}), so the Facebook100 ordering is
not a generic property of any reasonable graph; it depends on the
attribute structure that the GNN cost surface exploits.

\section{Cross-campus regression coefficients}
\label{app:regression}

The full per-$(\mathrm{policy}, R_0, \mathrm{predictor})$ univariate
slope, intercept, Pearson correlation, and small-sample $R^2$ are
stored in \texttt{results/cross\_campus\_regression.parquet} ($128$
rows: $4$ policies $\times$ $4\;R_0$ $\times$ $8$ predictors). The
campus-level descriptive statistics that drive the regression are in
\texttt{results/campus\_structural\_stats.csv}. The most robust
qualitative finding (consistent across all four policies and all
$R_0 \geq 1.5$) is that AUC rises with global clustering coefficient
and edge density and falls with node count, with $|r| > 0.87$ in
every cell at $R_0 \geq 1.5$. With $n = 5$ campuses this is
descriptive direction rather than an inferential test.
